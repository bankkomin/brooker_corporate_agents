# Permanent Establishment under the Thailand–Singapore DTA

## Quick-answer aliases (for retrieval)

- **Q: What is the most actionable mitigation for FOF place-of-effective-management risk?** A: Assign FOF bank-account **signing authority to a non-Thai-resident**, and exercise that authority **outside Thailand** (per Timblick & Partners opinion 29 Dec 2025, para 4(d)).
- **Q: How do we reduce PE / place-of-effective-management exposure?** A: Same — offshore signer + offshore signing; no day-to-day management onshore in Thailand.
- **Q: Does a Thai-resident director alone create PE risk?** A: **No**, residency alone does not. The risk is day-to-day management onshore (DTA Article 5(2)(a)).
- **Q: What is the preparatory/auxiliary carve-out?** A: **DTA Article 5(4)(e)** — IR / BDO activities are preparatory and do not create PE.
- **Q: What is the dependent-agent PE rule?** A: **DTA Article 5(5)** — habitually concluding contracts on behalf of the fund creates PE.

## Summary

A **permanent establishment ("PE")** in Thailand is the trigger that would make
[[fof-thai-tax-regulatory-exemption|FOF's profits]] taxable in Thailand. The Timblick &
Partners opinion of 29 December 2025 analyses PE risk under the **Double Tax Agreement
between Thailand and Singapore ("DTA")** and concludes that, on the facts assumed, FOF's
onshore activities should *not* create a PE — but flags one material risk: the **place of
effective management**.

## Definition — Article 5 of the DTA

**Article 5(2)** — "permanent establishment" includes especially: (a) a place of
management; (b) a branch; (c) an office; (d) a factory; (e) a workshop; (f) a mine, oil/gas
well, quarry or other place of extraction; (g) a warehouse.

**Article 5(4) — the preparatory/auxiliary carve-out.** A PE is deemed *not* to include a
fixed place of business maintained solely for storage/display/delivery, stock-keeping,
purchasing or collecting information, or — under **Article 5(4)(e)** — "carrying on... any
other activity of a **preparatory or auxiliary character**", or any combination of (a)–(e)
that is overall preparatory or auxiliary.

**Article 5(5) — dependent agent PE.** A person (other than an independent agent) acting
in Thailand on behalf of a Singapore enterprise creates a PE if that person **habitually
exercises authority to conclude contracts** in the enterprise's name — unless the
activities are limited to the Article 5(4) carve-out.

## How PE Triggers Thai Tax

Under **Article 7(1)** of the DTA, an enterprise's profits are taxable only in its home
state *unless* it carries on business through a PE in the other state; if it does, profits
attributable to that PE may be taxed there. Combined with **Section 66 paragraph 2** and
**Section 76 bis** of the Revenue Code, a Thai PE makes FOF's attributable profits subject
to Thai corporate income tax.

## The Key Risk — Place of Effective Management

The opinion's principal warning: the **place of effective management** — where key
management and commercial decisions necessary for the conduct of FOF's business as a whole
are *in substance* made — may itself constitute a PE under **Article 5(2)(a)** (a place of
management). If BTC's Thai-resident director makes day-to-day management decisions for FOF
in Thailand, FOF's profits may become taxable in Thailand. This risk is developed in
[[thai-resident-director-pe-risk]].

The Thai Revenue Department may weigh: where board meetings are held; where the CEO and
senior executives carry on their activities; where senior day-to-day management occurs;
where headquarters are located; which country's law governs the entity; and where
accounting records are kept.

## Why It Matters

The preparatory/auxiliary carve-out (Article 5(4)(e)) is what protects the marketing-style
activities of BROOK's personnel — see [[reverse-solicitation-thai-sec]] and
[[bdo-venture-capital-events-thai-intermediary]]. Conversely, decision-making and
contract-execution activity onshore breaks the carve-out and risks a PE.

## Agent Notes

"Preparatory or auxiliary" is the load-bearing phrase. Any proposed onshore activity should
be tested against Article 5(4)(e): if it involves decision-making, negotiation, or
execution it is unlikely to qualify.
