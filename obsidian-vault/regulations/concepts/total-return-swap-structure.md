# Total Return Swap (TRS) Structure

## Summary

A **Total Return Swap (TRS)** is a derivative in which a **Total Return Payer** passes the
total economic return of an underlying asset — interest plus price appreciation — to a
**Total Return Receiver**, in exchange for a financing-rate cash flow (historically LIBOR
plus a spread). This folder holds two draft deal structures (dated 6 January 2023,
attributed to an article provided by "WCP") illustrating how a TRS-style arrangement can
be used as a hedging / risk-management (การป้องกันความเสี่ยง) tool on listed shares.

## How the Draft Structures Work

**Structure A — direct.** An Investor buys, holds and owns the shares; the Individual
sells and delivers shares to the Investor and receives sale proceeds held in **escrow**.
The Investor holds full rights and benefits (dividends, voting). Later the Investor sells
the shares in the market — whenever the Individual approves, or after a 1-year expiry,
whichever is earlier — transfers the gain to the Individual and keeps an amount equal to
the escrowed proceeds; escrowed money is then released.

**Structure B — via SPV with structured notes.** The Individual issues structured notes
to an Investor, who sets up a special-purpose vehicle (SPV). The SPV buys shares via a
crossing process and the Individual delivers shares to the SPV, with sale proceeds locked
up for an agreed period (**not more than one year**). The SPV later sells the shares
(again, on Individual approval or 1-year expiry, whichever first); the Investor sells the
SPV back to the Individual, receives a fee return plus the locked-up proceeds to offset
the structured notes, and the Individual then owns and liquidates the SPV.

## Why It Matters

These are **draft structures only** and not a regulatory text — they record how a TRS
arrangement may be used to obtain economic exposure to, or hedge, a listed shareholding
while another party retains legal ownership for a defined period (capped at one year).
Such arrangements carry disclosure, takeover and connected-transaction implications under
Thai SEC and SET rules that must be assessed before use.
