# General Mandate Capital Increase

## Summary

A **General Mandate** (การเพิ่มทุนแบบมอบอำนาจทั่วไป) is a capital-increase method
under which a SET-listed company asks its shareholders, in advance, to authorise the
board of directors to issue and allocate new shares at the board's discretion, in such
amounts and on such occasions as the board deems appropriate — without convening a
fresh shareholder meeting each time funds are needed. It contrasts with the conventional
**specific-purpose capital increase** (การเพิ่มทุนแบบกำหนดวัตถุประสงค์), where each
issuance is approved by shareholders for a stated purpose.

The mechanism is governed by the SET Board's notification on the disclosure rules and
practices for capital increases of listed companies (B.E. 2554 / 2011, dated 15 March
2011) and Circular Bor.Jor.(Wor.) 47/2554, together with SEC Capital Market Supervisory
Board notification ThorJor.28/2551 on the offering of newly-issued shares.

## How It Works

Shareholders approve, at a general meeting, a ceiling number of shares the board may
later issue. When the board exercises the mandate it must make a further, complete
disclosure to the market under the standard capital-increase rules and file Form 53-4
(capital increase report) and Form 53-4-1 (issuance and allocation of new shares).

## Key Metrics / Thresholds

Caps are expressed as a percentage of paid-up capital at the date the board resolves to
table the General Mandate agenda:

| Allocation type | Cap |
|-----------------|-----|
| Rights Offering (RO) — existing shareholders pro rata | not more than 30% |
| Public Offering (PO) — general public | not more than 20% |
| Private Placement (PP) — specific persons | not more than 10% |
| Combined total (all methods) | not more than 30% |
| Combined Non-RO (PO + PP) | not more than 20% |

- **Offer price:** unrestricted, but must not qualify as a "low price" offering under SEC
  rules (notification SorJor.39/2551 on calculating offer price / market price).
- **Allocation window:** the mandate must be exercised by the next AGM, or by the legal
  deadline for holding the AGM, whichever is earlier.

## Why It Matters

A General Mandate gives a listed issuer speed and flexibility — fundraising can be
completed in roughly 4–10 weeks because no further shareholder meeting is required —
while shareholders retain protection through the percentage ceilings and a foreseeable
one-year dilution estimate.
