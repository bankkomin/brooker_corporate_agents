# Merger & Acquisition Methods and Tax Treatment (Thailand)

## Summary

Based on the **Merger Tax Manual** (คู่มือภาษีการควบรวมกิจการ, as of November 2015), this
article summarises the structures available for combining businesses in Thailand and the
tax considerations attached to each. A "merger" (การควบรวมกิจการ) means two or more legal
entities transferring their entire business, assets, or shares to one another, at the
shareholder or company level. A transfer of only part of a business is generally a takeover
rather than a merger.

## Three M&A Structures

1. **Share Acquisition (การได้มาซึ่งหุ้น)** — Acquiring shares of the target. Consideration
   may be **cash** (payment in cash) or **other property** (payment in kind), including a
   **share swap**, settling the seller's debt, or setting up a holding company. In a share
   swap the acquirer's shareholders become shareholders of the target.
2. **Asset / Business Acquisition (การได้มาซึ่งสินทรัพย์หรือกิจการ)** — Acquiring the
   target's assets or business directly rather than its shares.
3. **Amalgamation / Consolidation (การควบบริษัท)** — Statutory combination of companies
   under the Civil and Commercial Code (Sections 1238–1243, for limited companies) and the
   Public Limited Companies Act B.E. 2535.

Terminology from the manual: a transfer of assets and liabilities to the receiving entity
is a **statutory merger**; merging the transferor into the receiver's group as a subsidiary
is a **subsidiary merger**; merging a subsidiary of the receiver into the transferor is a
**reverse subsidiary merger**.

## Classifying Combinations

- **By volume of assets transferred:** Entire Business Transfer (โอนกิจการทั้งหมด) vs
  Partial Business Transfer (โอนกิจการบางส่วน).
- **By origin:** voluntary mergers vs mergers required by law (e.g. under the Financial
  Institutions Business Act B.E. 2551, the Securities and Exchange Act B.E. 2535, the
  Non-Life Insurance Act B.E. 2535).

## Tax Treatment

The manual focuses on tax under the Revenue Code and special laws. Combinations that
qualify for tax privileges fall into three cases: **entire business transfer**, **partial
business transfer**, and **cases with a specific statutory provision**. The manual covers
structure and tax only — practitioners must separately check partnership/company law,
public-company law, securities law, real-property law, and labour law for each deal.

## Why It Matters

Choice of M&A structure (share vs asset vs amalgamation) drives both the legal process and
the tax outcome of any Brooker corporate combination. Entire-business-transfer structures
can unlock Revenue Code tax privileges that an asset-by-asset acquisition does not.
