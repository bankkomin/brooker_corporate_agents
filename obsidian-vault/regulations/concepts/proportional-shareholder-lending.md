# Pro-Rata Shareholder Lending — Governance Practice

## Summary

A recurring practitioner question is whether, when a listed company lends money to a
jointly-owned subsidiary or joint venture, each shareholder must lend **in proportion to
its shareholding (การปล่อยเงินกู้ตามสัดส่วนการถือหุ้น)**. The research note in this folder
concludes that there is **no specific Thai regulation mandating pro-rata shareholder
lending** — it is treated as a matter of **good corporate governance** rather than a
hard legal requirement.

## Key Metrics

- **No dedicated rule:** the source note states plainly that pro-rata lending appears not
  to be required by any specific regulation; it is observed in practice as a fair-treatment
  / good-governance norm. The folder collects market *examples* (case studies from listed
  companies — BTS, Thaicom/THCOM, Forth Vending) rather than a governing notification.
- **Where the rules do bite:** when a shareholder loan is extended to or from a *connected
  person*, the transaction falls under the connected-transaction rules — board or
  shareholder approval and disclosure apply per the size tiers. Financial assistance to a
  related party is explicitly within scope of both the legacy framework
  ([[connected-transaction-rules]]) and the 2026 reform ([[material-transactions-rpt-2026-reform]]).

> Coverage is marked low: the underlying material is a brief research note plus example
> filings, not a primary regulation. The takeaway is the *absence* of a specific rule.

## Historical Context

The question typically arises in group-financing structures where a listed parent and
minority co-investors must fund a joint venture. Absent a pro-rata mandate, the governance
expectation is that any non-pro-rata or related-party lending be fairly priced, properly
approved, and disclosed under the connected-transaction regime.
