# Capital Reduction to Eliminate Share Discount and Deficit

## Summary

When a listed company carries a **share discount** (ส่วนต่ำมูลค่าหุ้น — shares previously
issued below par) and an accumulated **deficit** (retained-earnings deficit), it may
restructure its equity through one or more rounds of **capital reduction** (การลดทุน) to
absorb those negative components. This folder documents an accounting question put to the
Federation of Accounting Professions (FAP) on **1 September 2025** — whether a capital
reduction should **offset the share discount before, or after, the retained-earnings
deficit** — illustrated with a WAVE Plc pro-forma worked example.

## The WAVE Worked Example

Using WAVE Plc pro-forma figures as of 30 June 2025 (par THB 0.50/share):

- **Round 1 — offset share discount.** Paid-up capital reduced by THB 4,147m (a **-72.4%**
  cut to paid-up shares), eliminating the THB 4,147m share discount.
- **Round 2 — offset retained-earnings deficit.** A further THB 897m reduction (**-56.8%**
  of remaining paid-up shares), eliminating the THB 897m deficit.
- Result: paid-up capital falls from THB 5,728m across 11.46bn shares to THB 684m across
  1.37bn shares — **~11.93% of the original paid-up capital remaining** — while total
  shareholders' equity (THB 521m) and total assets are unchanged. Book value per share
  rises from THB 0.05 to THB 0.38 (on shareholders' equity).

The reference cited is FAP guidance no. 433/2548 dated 10 November 2005.

## Why It Matters

A capital reduction does not change total equity or total assets — it re-labels the
components, removing the share discount and deficit so the company can, for example,
resume paying dividends or improve its book-value-per-share optics. The sequencing
(discount before deficit) affects the recorded figures at each interim step. Significant
equity erosion also interacts with SET trading-sign rules — see [[c-caution-sign]].
