# Ministry of Finance Regulations on Government-Agency Share Trading

## Summary

This topic covers the **Ministry of Finance Regulations on the Sale and Purchase of Shares
by Government Agencies** (ระเบียบกระทรวงการคลัง ว่าด้วยการจำหน่ายหุ้นและซื้อหุ้นของ
ส่วนราชการ) — the original B.E. 2535 (1992) regulation and its three later amendments
(No. 2, B.E. 2546; No. 3, B.E. 2546; No. 4, B.E. 2550). They govern how a Thai government
agency (ส่วนราชการ) may dispose of or acquire shares.

## Key Points — B.E. 2535 Regulation

- Issued under Section 24, paragraph 5 of the Budget Procedure Act B.E. 2502, by the
  Minister of Finance with Cabinet approval.
- **"Shares"** is defined broadly — shares in a limited company, in a state enterprise, or
  partnership interests, or shares pooled in any other undertaking.
- A share disposal under this regulation is **exempt** from the Prime Minister's Office
  regulation on disposing of state-owned businesses or shares (B.E. 2504).
- Administrative authority runs through the Permanent Secretary of the Ministry of
  Finance and the Director-General of the Comptroller General's Department.

The three amendments (B.E. 2546 ×2 and B.E. 2550) progressively refined the procedure;
the consolidated effect is the regime currently governing government-agency share
transactions.

## Related — Bank of Thailand Supervised Financial Businesses

The same `ระเบียบกระทรวงการคลัง` folder includes a reference sheet on **financial
businesses supervised by the Bank of Thailand (ธปท.)** — commercial banks (including
retail banks, foreign-bank subsidiaries and branches), finance companies, credit foncier
companies, representative offices, asset-management companies, non-banks and e-payment
operators — together with entities the BoT inspects on behalf of the Ministry of Finance
(specialised financial institutions, the Thai Asset Management Corporation, the National
Credit Bureau). It is a supervisory-perimeter reference rather than a binding rule.

## Relevance

Peripheral to Brooker's listed-company operations — relevant mainly where a counterparty
or co-investor is a Thai government agency, since such a party's share transactions are
governed by these MoF regulations rather than ordinary company law. The BoT
supervised-business list is useful context for classifying financial counterparties.
