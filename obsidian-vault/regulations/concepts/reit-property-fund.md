# REITs & Property Funds (ทรัสต์เพื่อการลงทุนในอสังหาริมทรัพย์)

## Summary

A **REIT** (Real Estate Investment Trust, ทรัสต์เพื่อการลงทุนในอสังหาริมทรัพย์) is a pooled
real-estate investment vehicle constituted as a **trust** — a "pool of assets"
(กองทรัพย์สิน) held by a **trustee** (ทรัสตี). A REIT is **not a juristic person**. The
trust originator becomes the **REIT manager**, who offers trust units and manages the
fund, while the trustee holds and safeguards the assets and oversees the manager. REITs
are the SEC-promoted successor structure to the older **property funds** (กองทุนรวม
อสังหาริมทรัพย์). This concept summarises the SEC REIT framework from the SEC investor
brochure dated March 2015.

## REIT Structure & Key Requirements

- The trust deed (สัญญาก่อตั้งทรัสต์) follows the **Trust for Transactions in the Capital
  Market Act B.E. 2550 (2007)** and related SEC notifications.
- An applicant files an offering application with the **SEC Office**, which decides within
  **45 days** of receiving a complete application. Required documents include the trust
  deed, the offering registration statement (filing), a property valuation report, and a
  trustee certification letter.
- **Size** — paid-up capital after the offering must be at least **THB 500 million**.
- REIT trust **units must be listed on the Stock Exchange of Thailand**.
- Units may be divided into multiple **tranches** under prescribed rules.
- A REIT may invest in **all types of real estate** for the purpose of generating rental
  income, including **overseas real estate**, provided the business is not contrary to law
  or morality.

## Fees (SEC, 2015)

- Offering-application fee: **THB 100,000** per application.
- Filing fee: **0.01%** of the total value of trust units offered.
- REIT-manager approval: **THB 250,000** per application (and per renewal).
- Trustee licence application: **THB 30,000** per application.

## Property Funds — Freehold vs Leasehold

The older property-fund regime distinguished two types by the nature of the real-estate
right held:

- **Freehold property fund** — the fund **owns** the real estate outright; on dissolution
  the asset can be sold, giving unitholders potential **capital gain**.
- **Leasehold property fund** — the fund holds a **lease** (commonly capped around 30
  years); the right **expires** at the end of the lease term, so income is essentially a
  return of and on the lease and there is typically no terminal capital gain.

## Why It Matters

REITs are the principal listed vehicle for monetising income-producing real estate in
Thailand and a relevant structure for any Brooker real-estate or yield-oriented strategy.
The THB 500M minimum size, mandatory SET listing, and the freehold/leasehold distinction
materially affect deal structuring and expected unitholder returns.
