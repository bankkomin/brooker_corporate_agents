## 10:20 · @U-TEST · proposal: none
**Q:** what is LCR
**A:** The Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR) is a regulatory metric designed to ensure that a financial institution holds sufficient High-Quality Liquid Assets (HQLA) to survive a significant stress scenario over a 30-day period.

**Definition and Formula**
The LCR is calculated as the ratio of the stock of HQLA to total net cash outflows over the next 30 calendar days, expressed as a percentage:
$$ \text{LCR} = \left( \frac{\text{Stock of HQLA}}{\text{Total Net Cash Outflows over 30 days}} \right) \times 100\% $$

**Regulatory Threshold**
The minimum regulatory requirement is 100% [Source: liquidity-policy-2026.md]. An LCR of 100% indicates that the bank holds exactly enough liquid assets to cover projected net outflows during the stress period. Any ratio above 100% represents a liquidity buffer exceeding the minimum requirement.

**Components**
*   **HQLA (High-Quality Liquid Assets):** These are assets that can be converted into cash immediately or within a short timeframe without significant loss of value. They are typically categorized into tiers (e.g., Level 1, Level 2A, Level 2B) with specific haircut values applied to reflect their liquidity risk.
*   **Net Cash Outflows:** This is the estimated total cash leaving the institution minus cash inflows, based on stressed assumptions for deposit withdrawals, loan draws, and other contractual obligations over the 30-day horizon.

**Purpose**
The LCR ensures that banks maintain an adequate stock of unencumbered, high-quality liquid assets to meet their liquidity needs under an acute short-term stress scenario, thereby reducing the risk of a liquidity crisis and the need for external bailouts.
