# Stablecoins as a Digital Dollar

## Summary

Stablecoins recur as the most "product-market-fit" use case in crypto: dollar-pegged
tokens that demonstrate demand for a digital dollar. The collection covers their three
designs, their growth, and the regulatory and design risks exposed in 2022–2023.

## Three Designs

| Type | Backing | Examples | Risk profile |
|------|---------|----------|--------------|
| Fiat-collateralised (centralised) | Bank reserves / T-bills | USDC, USDT, BUSD | Economically safest; censorship/freeze risk |
| Crypto-collateralised (overcollateralised) | On-chain crypto collateral | DAI | Robust, tested March 2020 & May 2022; hard to scale |
| Algorithmic / endogenous | Sister token mint-burn | UST (failed) | Prone to death spiral |

## Key Points

- Growing supply "demonstrates demand for a digital dollar," but stablecoins remain a
  tiny fraction of global M1 (a16z).
- **Regulatory risk is real:** US regulators forced Paxos to stop issuing BUSD in 2023,
  collapsing BUSD's market cap ~54% on BNB Chain within a quarter.
- After Terra, the research consensus is that scalable decentralised stablecoins must be
  overcollateralised; purely algorithmic designs are too fragile (see
  [[algorithmic-stablecoin-death-spiral]]).
- Centralised stablecoins (USDC/USDT) are "the linchpin of current crypto" but their
  freezability is cited as an unresolved decentralisation gap.
