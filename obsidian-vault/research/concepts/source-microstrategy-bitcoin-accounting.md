# Source Summary — MicroStrategy Bitcoin Accounting & Custodian Contracting (2020-2021)

**As of: 3 February 2021 (accounting memo as-of date)**

## Document Overview

Three supporting documents from MicroStrategy's corporate-Bitcoin program: a **Bitcoin
Accounting Treatment & Tax Considerations** memo, a **Contractual Considerations for
Digital Asset Custodians** checklist, and a co-authored MicroStrategy & **Deloitte**
paper, "Corporates Investing in Crypto". Together they cover the accounting, custody, and
cross-functional dimensions of the [[corporate-bitcoin-treasury-playbook]].

## Key Findings

**Accounting & tax**
- MicroStrategy concluded its Bitcoin investment is an **indefinite-lived intangible
  asset** under FASB ASC 350 (Intangibles — Goodwill and Other).
- **Impairment losses** are recorded when fair value falls below carrying value and
  **cannot be reversed** on a subsequent price increase — an asymmetric accounting
  treatment.
- On sale, gain/loss is recorded under ASC 610 (Other Income) based on fair value at the
  sale date. The memo works through seven accounting/tax questions (control, classification,
  subsequent measurement, unit of account, sales, disclosures, tax).

**Custodian contracting checklist**
- Considerations include counterparty jurisdiction and licensing (trust companies
  licensed in South Dakota, Nevada, New York), **segregated vs omnibus custody**,
  brokerage/lending ancillary services, and service-level commitments.

**Deloitte paper — "Corporates Investing in Crypto"**
- Notes MicroStrategy's >$1B total Bitcoin purchases in 2020, characterized as an
  investment to "provide the opportunity for better returns and preserve the value of
  capital over time compared to holding cash".
- Stresses cross-organization collaboration (treasury, accounting, tax, controls) and the
  challenges of accounting divergence under the intangible-asset model.

## Relevance

Surfaces the key accounting friction of a corporate Bitcoin treasury — the
non-reversible-impairment model under ASC 350 — and the custody-contracting due diligence
required. Both are direct considerations for Brooker's digital-asset treasury reporting.
