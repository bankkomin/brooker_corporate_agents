# Crypto Fund Structuring & Licensing

## Summary

A recurring theme across the VC-pitchbook, contracts, and compliance research: how a
crypto / digital-asset fund is **legally structured, domiciled, licensed, and
documented**. The collection includes a Hong Kong regulatory guide
([[source-crypto-funds-hong-kong-overview]]) and several worked fund examples — Kazea
(BVI), Spartan Venture Fund II (Cayman) — that illustrate common patterns.

## Common Structural Patterns

| Element | Typical choice (observed) |
|---------|---------------------------|
| Domicile | BVI (Kazea) or Cayman Islands exempted LP (Spartan VF II) |
| Vehicle | Limited partnership with a separate GP entity |
| GP commitment | ~2% of commitments (Kazea) |
| Management fee | ~2% p.a. during investment period, step-down thereafter |
| Carried interest | 20% standard; higher tiers (30%) above performance hurdles |
| Term | ~6–8 years plus one-year extensions |
| Minimum commitment | US$200,000 (both Kazea and Spartan VF II) |
| Offering documents | PPM, Limited Partnership Agreement, Subscription Agreement |

## Licensing — the Hong Kong Lens

Hong Kong's regime turns on whether the fund's assets are "SF Assets" (securities or
futures). A **Type 9 (Asset Management) licence** is required to manage SF Assets; the
**Type 9 VA Licence** regime (October 2019) expands this for virtual assets and is
compulsory once virtual assets reach ≥10% of portfolio gross asset value. Pure utility
tokens are "virtual commodities", not securities.

## Why It Matters

These structures are reference templates for Brooker's own VCC fund platform and
