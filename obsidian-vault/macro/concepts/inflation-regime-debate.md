# The Inflation Regime Debate — Transitory vs Sticky

## Summary

A recurring debate running through the 2022-2024 macro material: is the post-COVID
inflation spike **transitory** (a supply-shock distortion to a structurally
disinflationary world) or **sticky** (a persistent regime requiring sustained tight
policy)? The macro area carries voices on both sides.

## The Disinflationary / Transitory Camp

- **[[global-macro-investor]] / Raoul Pal** — strongly in the transitory camp. The
  1970s inflation was driven by the *demographic demand shock* of Boomers entering the
  workforce; today's demographics are the reverse. "Inflation is yesterday's news";
  the business cycle drives inflation (CPI lags the ISM by ~7 months), so a growth
  slowdown collapses inflation. See [[demographics-as-destiny]] and
  [[business-cycle-cowbell]].
- **[[rosenberg-research]] / David Rosenberg** — *Inflation Still Transitory!* (June
  2022) leaned on Bob Farrell's Rule #9 ("when all the experts agree, something else
  happens") to call against the consensus that high inflation would persist.

## The "Not Dead Yet" Camp

- **Julian Brigden (Macro Insiders)** — *Inflation: Not Dead Yet* (July 2024) argued
  the opposite into 2024: negative base effects were dropping out, rising goods prices
  could more than offset flat services inflation, and loose financial conditions
  pointed to headline CPI re-accelerating. He warned this was a "narrative breach" for
  a market positioned for sequential rate cuts (the June 2024 BofA survey showed 84%
  expecting a soft/no landing).

## Why It Matters

The inflation call drives everything downstream: the path of bond yields, the timing
of rate cuts ("cowbell"), and risk sentiment. The structural backdrop — ageing
demographics, automation, debt — is the disinflationary anchor; the cyclical and
fiscal forces are the sticky-side risk.
