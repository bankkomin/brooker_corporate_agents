# Demographics as Destiny

## Summary

A recurring thesis across both major research voices in the macro area: **ageing
populations and collapsing workforce growth are a structural, near-irreversible drag
on GDP growth** — and therefore on debt sustainability, inflation, and asset returns.
It is megatrend #2 in the [[the-seven-megatrends]] of [[absolute-return-partners]] and
one of the three secular drivers (debt, deflation, demographics) of
[[global-macro-investor]]'s original 2005 thesis and [[the-everything-code]].

## The Core Argument

- Workforce growth has always been a key driver of GDP growth; a shrinking workforce
  drags GDP lower. The OECD will lose ~150 million workers to retirement by 2050; 42
  countries already had negative working-age population growth as of end-2019.
- Japan, South Korea, Italy, Spain and Germany are the most affected; the US is the
  least affected major economy (workforce still grows ~0.27% p.a.). Globally, growth is
  positive (~0.67% p.a.) but almost entirely due to Africa (+2.56% p.a.).
- China's old-age dependency ratio is projected to rise from ~17% to over 53% by 2060.
  France took ~150 years for its average age to double; China and Thailand took ~20.

## Demographics → Debt → Deflation

Lower workforce growth means slower GDP, decelerating corporate earnings, shrinking
income-tax revenue, and rising elderly-care costs. Governments plug the gap with debt
— see [[debt-supercycle]]. ARP and GMI both argue ageing is structurally
**disinflationary** (the Japan precedent), against the counter-view that retirees
consume without producing.

## Robots Are Demographics

Raoul Pal's 2022 deep-dive "Robots Are Demographics" reframes the problem: **robots
and AI are the synthetic replacement for the missing population**. The fixes for
falling workforce growth — another baby boom, or immigration — are unavailable
(technology suppresses wages; immigration is politically unacceptable). Productivity
via automation is the only lever, which connects this thesis directly to
[[the-exponential-age]]. The investing "Catch 22": invest and you accelerate it; don't
invest and it happens anyway without you.

## Why It Matters for Markets

- The "Spending Wave" (Harry Dent) correlates demographic peaks with inflation-adjusted
  equity returns; retiring Boomers (who own ~60% of US wealth) turn from a tailwind into
  a headwind for equities.
- Demographics underpin the [[the-retirement-crisis]] — under-saved Boomers and Gen X.
- Slower potential growth keeps trend interest rates structurally low.
