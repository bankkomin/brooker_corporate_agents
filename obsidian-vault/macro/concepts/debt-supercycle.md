# The Debt Supercycle

## Summary

The debt supercycle thesis holds that the developed world is in the **late, "panic"
stage** of a multi-decade build-up of debt that can no longer be serviced by economic
growth. It is megatrend #1 of [[absolute-return-partners]] and is structurally
identical to the debt mechanics inside [[the-everything-code]]. Total global debt
exceeded **US$300 trillion** by 2022; the IMF called debt levels "dangerously high."

## Late-Stage Characteristics

ARP identifies the markers of a late-stage debt supercycle: low bond yields, low risk
premia, decelerating productivity, anaemic GDP growth, and plentiful misallocated
capital. The cycle moves through a *bubble stage* (debt grows faster than income,
equities rally, the curve flattens, policy is too lenient) into a *panic stage*, where
monetary policy stops working — what 1930s central bankers called "pushing on a string."

## How Debt Supercycles End

There is **no benign exit**. Debt is destroyed either through **default** (disruptive,
as in 2008) or through **inflation** (less disruptive, as c.1980). ARP's 2022 view was
that inflation was — conveniently — doing the job. The challenge for central banks is
to destroy as much real debt as possible without losing control of inflation.

## The GMI Framing — The Debt Trap

[[global-macro-investor]] frames the same phenomenon as the "debt trap": once private
debt exceeds ~100-120% of GDP, all trend GDP growth (~1.75%) is consumed servicing
interest. Rates *must* sit at or below GDP growth; positive real rates "bankrupt the
system, and fast." The only exit is debt monetisation every time growth or assets fall
— hence [[currency-debasement]].

## Investment Themes (ARP)

ARP attaches three investment themes to this megatrend:
- **Out-of-Control Public Deficits** — see [[us-sovereign-debt-risk]]
- **Regulatory Arbitrage** — non-bank lending fills the gap left by Basel III
  (ARP estimates ~90% of US SME lending is now non-bank); strategies include
  Regulatory Capital Relief, middle-market lending, trade finance, specialty finance.
- **The Productivity Conundrum** — productivity has slowed despite robots, dragged by
  ageing, debt and poor infrastructure.
