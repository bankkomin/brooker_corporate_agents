# Monetary Inflation and the End of the 60:40 Portfolio

## Summary

"Monetary inflation" is [[crossborder-capital]]'s term for the **accelerating long-run
uptrend** in Global Liquidity that sits beneath the cyclical [[global-liquidity-cycle]].
The claim: liquidity *must* grow at least as fast as world debt, and because the public
sector dominates both debt issuance and bond-collateral supply, liquidity is set to
expand **6-8% per year on average — doubling every decade**. This is driven not by
active policy choices but by *non-decisions*: future spending commitments are largely
set in stone and "the frightening math follows automatically."

## Why It Is Inevitable

- The US$35 trillion (2024) US public-debt stock has ~6-year average maturity, so
  ~US$6 trillion must be **rolled every year**, on top of ~US$2 trillion of new
  issuance.
- Rolling and financing this requires monetisation, yield-curve control, and ever-larger
  private-sector balance-sheet capacity — all of which equal more liquidity.
- US "taxes" (Milton Friedman's rule: all government outlays are taxes) have de facto
  risen ~38% since 2019 via spending. Mandatory outlays (Social Security, Medicare,
  interest) drift relentlessly higher.

## Monetary Inflation ≠ Consumer Inflation

Monetary inflation does not translate one-to-one into high-street CPI. Since 1972,
Global Liquidity expanded ~9.1% p.a. while US CPI averaged ~4% — the gap absorbed by
productivity, technology and cheap goods. This overlaps closely with the
[[currency-debasement]] thesis: the inflation shows up first in *asset* prices.

## Investment Implication — Beyond 60:40

If monetary inflation runs 6-8% p.a., the traditional **60:40 equity/bond portfolio
breaks down**: bond returns cannot keep pace, and bonds lose their hedging property
because their returns become positively correlated with equities to changes in the
pace of monetary inflation. CrossBorder's prescription: replace ~20 percentage points
of bond exposure with explicit monetary-inflation hedges — **gold, TIPS, and
potentially Bitcoin** — plus quality stocks with pricing power. (Gold averaged 7.8%
p.a. since 1972; portfolios held 10-15% gold in the early 1980s vs under 1% today.)
