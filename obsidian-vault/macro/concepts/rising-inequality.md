# Rising Inequality and the Rise of Populism

## Summary

A cross-house thesis: the widening gap between rich and poor — and the populism it
breeds — is a structural drag on growth and political stability. It is megatrend #3 of
[[absolute-return-partners]] and a recurring theme in [[global-macro-investor]], where
it is framed as the social consequence of [[currency-debasement]].

## The Naked Facts (ARP)

- The top 10% has roughly doubled the wealth of the bottom 90%. The top 0.1% now holds
  as much US wealth as the bottom 90% — last seen in the 1930s.
- The top 1% captured ~27% of all global income growth from 1980-2016.
- Real wage growth since the 2008 crisis has been near-zero across major OECD
  economies (~-1% p.a. in the UK).
- McKinsey: 65-70% of households in 25 advanced economies had stagnant or falling real
  incomes in 2014 vs 2005.

## The Cycle of Discontent

ARP describes a self-reinforcing loop: discontent → populism → misallocated capital
(transfer payments over productivity-enhancing investment) → low/no productivity growth
→ low/no GDP growth. Populist vote share in Europe (~25%) and the developed-world
"Populism index" have only spiked twice in 120 years — the 1930s and now.

## The GMI Framing — The Debasement Connection

GMI ties inequality directly to [[currency-debasement]]: assets rise optically while
wages track only CPI, so anyone who does not already own assets gets *poorer in
asset-purchasing-power terms* each year — the "death of the American Dream" and the
root of populist anger. This connects to [[the-retirement-crisis]]: under-saved
Boomers (~US$150k median) and Gen X (~US$40k median with a decade to retirement).
