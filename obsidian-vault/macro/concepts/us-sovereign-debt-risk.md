# US Sovereign Debt Risk and Yield Curve Control

## Summary

A recurring thesis on the deteriorating US fiscal position and the policy response it
will force. [[crossborder-capital]] argues US sovereign assets are getting riskier:
term premia are mean-reverting higher and the yield curve will steepen — while
[[global-macro-investor]] argues the endgame is **yield curve control (YCC)**, the
suppression of bond yields below GDP growth.

## The Fiscal Arithmetic (CrossBorder, 2024)

- CBO/IMF projections put the US public-debt/GDP ratio on track toward **~175% by 2050**
  (and a scenario as high as ~230%), with annual public-debt growth above 15% of GDP.
- The US$35 trillion debt stock has ~6-year average maturity — ~US$6 trillion must be
  **rolled annually**, plus ~US$2 trillion of new issuance.
- Sensitivities: each +1% on the primary deficit adds ~22 points to 2050 debt/GDP; each
  +1% on average interest rates adds ~35 points.
- Term premia were deeply negative (~-115bp) and mean-reverting toward a +34bp average;
  CrossBorder targeted the 10-year yield testing ~5½% with a steepening curve.

## "Stealth" Yield Curve Control

CrossBorder argues a clever form of YCC is *already* underway: Treasury shifted
issuance toward bills (~22% of outstanding debt) and shortened the new-issuance tenor
by ~1.2 years since late 2022 — estimated to have already cut the 10-year yield by
80-100bp. Short-tenor issuance bought by banks is "pure monetisation."

## The GMI Framing — YCC as the Endgame

GMI frames YCC as the resolution of [[the-everything-code]]: governments cannot keep
compounding interest payments forever, so — like Japan (10-year yield pegged at 0.5%
vs 1.25% GDP) and the post-WWII US (rates capped at 2.5%, GDP at 5%) — they will peg
yields below growth. Once GDP exceeds interest rates by a margin, the debt problem is
"solved" and YCC "can continue ad infinitum."
