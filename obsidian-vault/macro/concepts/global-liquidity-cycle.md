# The Global Liquidity Cycle

## Summary

The Global Liquidity Cycle is the central framework of [[crossborder-capital]]
(Michael Howell). It holds that **changes in Global Liquidity — the capacity of
financial-sector balance sheets worldwide — are the dominant driver of asset prices**,
more important than corporate profits or policy interest rates. Liquidity is measured
monthly across ~90 economies. The cycle runs roughly **5-6 years**, set by the
periodicity of debt refinancing rather than the traditional 9-10 year capital cycle.

## The Four-Phase Cycle

| Phase | Term premia | Rate expectations | Yield curve |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Rebound | rising | rising | steepens / slightly flatter |
| Calm | rising | falling | significant steepening |
| Speculation | falling | modestly falling | pivots |
| Turbulence | falling | rising | flattening / inversion |

As of the September 2024 report, the cycle bottomed in **October 2022** and was
projected to **peak in late 2025**.

## What Drives It

Three factors matter most:

1. **The US Federal Reserve** — Fed liquidity drives world financial markets.
2. **The People's Bank of China** — PBoC liquidity drives the world *real* economy and
   commodities (leads the global GDP nowcast by ~3 months).
3. **The MOVE index** — US Treasury volatility sets the collateral *haircut* in repo
   markets, and therefore the size of the "collateral multiplier" (~1.53x in 2024).
   Howell argues the MOVE index is more important ex ante than the equity VIX.

## Markets as Refinancing Mechanisms

A key structural claim: financial markets have become **vast debt-refinancing
mechanisms, not capital-raising vehicles** — roughly 3 of every 4 capital-market deals
are refinancings. Wholesale banks operate on a *collateral multiplier* (borrowing
against Treasuries in repo), not the textbook deposit multiplier. This places US
Treasuries at the centre of the modern monetary system.

## The Stable Debt/Liquidity Ratio

There is a stable long-run relationship between world debt and the pool of Global
Liquidity, with a financial-stability threshold around **2.5x** for the Advanced
Economies. Breaching it up or down produces crises of monetary inflation or deflation
respectively.
