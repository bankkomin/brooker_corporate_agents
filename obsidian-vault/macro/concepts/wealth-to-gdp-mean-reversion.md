# Mean-Reversion of Wealth-to-GDP

## Summary

This [[absolute-return-partners]] thesis holds that **total wealth relative to GDP is
long-term stable** and *must* mean-revert. Within [[the-seven-megatrends]] it is
presented not as a standalone megatrend but as the **aggregate result** of the other
six. US wealth-to-GDP traded in a 350-400% range (mean ~380%) for decades, then climbed
to ~583% by Q3 2020 — roughly 50% above the long-term mean.

## Why It Is Stable

Wealth-to-GDP is a measure of capital-to-output — capital efficiency. Economic growth
theory holds the capital-to-output ratio stable at ~3-4x for any open-market economy,
independent of new technology. The steep 25-year rise is therefore "unsustainable and
can only be justified if economic growth theory can be proven wrong" — which no one has
done. ARP's arithmetic (Feb 2021): to revert to ~380% of a ~US$21 trillion GDP, US
wealth must fall from ~US$116.5 trillion toward ~US$80 trillion (a ~31% drop), with
equities and property taking most of the hit.

## Bubbles as a Catalyst

ARP notes that a bursting bubble could trigger the reversion. It identifies common
bubble characteristics (rising retail share, easy credit, ignored intrinsic value,
exploding call-option activity, irrational price drivers, ignored official warnings,
insiders distancing themselves) and — writing in early 2021 — flagged the joint Tesla /
Bitcoin run as a sign of "irrational exuberance." Note: ARP judged Big Tech earnings as
broadly *justifying* FAAMG valuations, locating bubble behaviour mainly in small,
unprofitable tech.

## Labour vs Capital

A linked claim from the inequality work: national income splits stably ~2/3 labour,
~1/3 capital. After decades of capital taking more than its share, labour is down to
55-60% — and this *too* must mean-revert, which would compress corporate profits and
favour value over growth.
