# Globalisation 2.0 — Trade Fragmentation

## Summary

"Globalisation 2.0" is the seventh and newest megatrend of [[absolute-return-partners]],
added in May 2022. It argues that the post-WWII wave of ever-rising international trade
has paused and is fragmenting into a more regional, more regulated configuration —
*not* ending, but reconfiguring with a new mix of winners and losers.

## The Core Argument

- International trade has stopped rising as a share of global GDP. The turning point was
  the 2008 Global Financial Crisis, which created "more losers than winners" in the
  developed world and fed the populism behind Brexit and Trump.
- G20 protectionist measures hit an all-time high (~5,560) between 2008 and 2016; WTO
  trade-uncertainty rose sharply. The Russia-Ukraine war added further momentum.
- ARP frames it as "Globalisation 2.0" rather than "Globalisation in Reverse" — the
  1980s-onward model was poorly balanced (high on finance and Big Tech, low on
  regulation); a pause used to fix that is "not a bad thing."

## Going Regional

Trade may be going *regional* rather than disappearing. RCEP — the Regional
Comprehensive Economic Partnership (10 ASEAN states plus Australia, China, Japan,
Korea, New Zealand) — came into force on 1 January 2022, covering ~2.2 billion people
and ~30% of global GDP, and is the first free-trade agreement linking China, Japan and
South Korea.

## Winners and Losers

- **Losers:** carbon-intensive manufacturers (EU carbon-credit scheme set to widen);
  Big Tech (EU regulatory campaign).
- **Winners:** anything green — lithium, copper and other green metals shipped in
  rising volume ("Could Chile become the Saudi Arabia of Globalisation 2.0?").
- ARP retired its old "Currency Wars / QE" investment theme with this paper, judging
  QT and rising inflation had ended that 40-year tailwind for risk assets.
