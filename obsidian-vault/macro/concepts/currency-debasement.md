# Currency Debasement and the Denominator Effect

## Summary

The debasement thesis holds that quantitative easing (QE) and central-bank balance-sheet
expansion are not primarily "liquidity injections" but the **purposeful monetisation of
government debt**, which debases fiat currency. Because most assets are priced in fiat,
debasing the *denominator* makes asset prices rise optically even when no real wealth is
created. It is the core mechanism inside [[the-everything-code]] and overlaps closely
with CrossBorder Capital's concept of [[monetary-inflation]].

## The Mechanism

- A government over ~200% of GDP in debt, growing at only ~1.75%, cannot service its
  debt from growth. QE delays the problem by monetising interest payments — "using the
  credit card to pay the interest on the credit card."
- The central-bank balance sheet rises to absorb this debt (with a ~3½-year lag in the
  GMI framing). The Fed balance sheet "is basically explained by the interest payments
  3½ years later."
- As fiat loses purchasing power versus assets, prices rise optically. This is why
  "value investing doesn't work" post-2008 and why P/E ratios become monetary
  indicators rather than valuations.

## Scarce vs Variable Supply

Debasement optically lifts **scarce-supply** assets — US equities (in short supply due
to buybacks and near-zero IPOs), gold, real estate, crypto — while **variable-supply**
assets such as commodities only follow the business cycle. Earnings rise with M2 growth,
which lags the balance sheet; the difference is P/E expansion.

## The Money Illusion

A central, socially-charged corollary: the wealth created is a "money illusion." In
real (asset-purchasing-power) terms the median household gets *poorer* — the median
American can buy ~89% less of the S&P 500 than in 1980 and ~26% less housing. Wages
track CPI; assets track debasement. This drives anger, populism and the "death of the
American Dream." See [[rising-inequality]].

## The Counter-Intuitive Dollar

A debased reserve currency *rises*, not falls — because most global debt is in dollars
and a dollar shortage forces a scramble. In the GMI framing the dollar lags the Fed
balance sheet by ~18 months. See [[dollar-dynamics]].

## Agent Notes

Debasement and liquidity are distinct in this material: liquidity is a *partial* asset
driver; debasement of the denominator is framed as the *key* driver. When reasoning
about asset prices in the GMI worldview, lead with debasement.
